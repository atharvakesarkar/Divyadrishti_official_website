About DivyaDrishti

DivyaDrishti is an environmental technology initiative focused on real-time air quality monitoring and awareness. The name combines “Divya” (divine) and “Drishti” (vision), reflecting our mission to provide a clear, informed view of the air we breathe. Through a smart blend of hardware sensors and a web-based dashboard, DivyaDrishti makes invisible pollutants visible to everyday citizens.

Our platform tracks pollutants like PM2.5, PM10, NO₂, and O₃, offering health recommendations and data visualizations to help users understand local air quality conditions. It empowers communities, schools, and authorities with actionable insights—driving environmental responsibility and smarter decisions.

Built by a team of passionate student innovators, DivyaDrishti is not just a tool, but a movement toward a cleaner, healthier future.

Our Mission

At DivyaDrishti, our mission is to empower communities with real-time environmental insights that drive action, awareness, and accountability. We aim to make air pollution visible, understandable, and solvable—through affordable, IoT-powered monitoring systems and a user-friendly digital dashboard.

We strive to bridge the gap between data and decision-making by delivering accurate, hyperlocal air quality information to individuals, institutions, and governments—enabling smarter choices for a healthier, sustainable future.

Why This Matters

Lancet Commission on Pollution and Health (2022)

Pollution caused 9 million deaths worldwide, including over 2.3 million in India (highest globally).
Air pollution is the top environmental health threat.

Read More
World Air Quality Report 2023 – IQAir

India is home to 39 of the world’s 50 most polluted cities.

Highlights severe PM2.5 levels across Indian cities, with Delhi ranked the most polluted capital.
Emphasizes the need for real-time monitoring, public awareness, and forecasting tools like DivyaDrishti.

Read More
WHO Global Air Quality Guidelines (2021)

Recommends PM2.5 annual mean exposure to be below 5 µg/m³.
Most Indian cities exceed safe limits by 4–10 times.
Underscores the need for continuous monitoring and public health communication.
